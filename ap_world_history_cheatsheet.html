Units 3–4B: Maritime Empires & Coerced Labor

c. 1450–1750
Maritime Empires & Trade Companies
  • Spanish: encomienda → hacienda system; silver mines at Potosí (Bolivia); Manila Galleons connected Americas-Asia
  • Portuguese: trading post empire (feitorias); controlled Indian Ocean early; Brazil colony
  • Dutch: VOC (Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie) = world's 1st joint-stock company; controlled spice trade in Indonesia
  • British: EIC (East India Company) in India; N. American colonies; plantation economy
  • French: fur trade in N. America; plantation colonies in Caribbean

Key Vocabulary — Trade
Joint-stock company — investors pool money; share profits and risks; e.g. VOC, EIC
Mercantilism — economic theory: colonies exist to enrich mother country; export raw materials, import finished goods; ↑ gold/silver
Triangular Trade — Europe (manufactured goods) → Africa (enslaved people) → Americas (raw materials) → back to Europe
Middle Passage — brutal transatlantic crossing for enslaved Africans; ~12M transported; ~15–20% mortality
Social Hierarchies (Casta System)

Spanish colonial hierarchy based on race/ancestry:

  • Peninsulares — born in Spain; highest status
  • Criollos — Spanish descent, born in Americas; couldn't hold top offices
  • Mestizos — Spanish + indigenous
  • Mulattos — Spanish + African
  • Indigenous — conquered peoples
  • Enslaved Africans — lowest, no legal rights
Resistance to Slavery & Colonialism
  • Maroon communities — escaped enslaved people formed free settlements (e.g. Jamaica, Brazil)
  • Stono Rebellion (1739) — largest slave revolt in colonial S. Carolina
  • Syncretic religions — blended African + Catholic: Vodou (Haiti), Candomblé (Brazil), Santería (Cuba)
  • Maintaining African culture — language, music, stories preserved despite suppression

Units 5–6B: Imperialism, Migration & Resistance

c. 1750–1900
Rationales for Imperialism
  • Economic: raw materials, new markets, cheap labor
  • Political: nationalism, strategic naval bases, competition between European powers
  • Ideological: Social Darwinism ("survival of fittest" applied to races), "White Man's Burden" (Kipling), "civilizing mission" (France), Christian missionary work
Key Events & Vocab
Berlin Conference (1884–85) — Europeans divided Africa w/o African input; "Scramble for Africa"; nearly all continent colonized by 1914
Social Darwinism — misapplication of Darwin's evolution to justify racial hierarchy and imperialism
Extraterritoriality — foreigners exempt from local law; imposed on China via unequal treaties after Opium Wars
Sphere of influence — region where one country has dominant power without direct colonial control
Economic imperialism — control through trade, debt, and infrastructure; no formal colony; e.g. British in Argentina
Unequal treaties — forced on China after Opium Wars; opened ports, gave extraterritoriality, ceded territory

Units 7–8: World Wars, Cold War & Decolonization

c. 1900–present
World War I (1914–1918)
  • MAIN causes: Militarism, Alliances (Triple Entente vs. Triple Alliance), Imperialism, Nationalism
  • Spark: assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand (Sarajevo, 1914)
  • Total war: trench warfare, poison gas, machine guns, tanks, airplanes; home front mobilized
  • Armenian Genocide (1915–16): Ottoman Turks killed ~1.5M Armenians; first modern genocide
  • Treaty of Versailles (1919): punished Germany (war guilt clause, reparations, disarmament, territory lost) → resentment → Hitler
Interwar Period (1918–1939)
  • Great Depression: 1929 stock crash → global unemployment → governments intervened (New Deal-USA, Five Year Plans-USSR)
  • Rise of fascism: Hitler (Germany), Mussolini (Italy), Tojo (Japan) → extreme nationalism, authoritarianism, racism
  • Appeasement: Britain/France allowed Hitler to take territory hoping to avoid war → backfired
World War II (1939–1945)
  • Axis: Germany, Italy, Japan vs. Allies: USA, Britain, USSR, France
  • Holocaust: systematic murder of 6M Jews + 5M others (Roma, disabled, LGBTQ, Slavs)
  • Total war: firebombing of Dresden/Tokyo; atomic bombs on Hiroshima & Nagasaki (Aug 1945)
  • ~70–85M total dead — deadliest conflict in history
  • UN founded 1945 → Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)
Mass Atrocities (List These!)
  • Armenian Genocide (1915) — Ottoman Empire
  • Holodomor (1932–33) — Soviet-caused famine in Ukraine (~5M dead)
  • Holocaust (1941–45) — Nazi Germany (6M Jews)
  • Chinese Cultural Revolution (1966–76) — Mao's purge (millions)
  • Cambodian Genocide (1975–79) — Khmer Rouge (~2M dead)
  • Rwandan Genocide (1994) — Hutu vs. Tutsi (~800,000 in 100 days)
Cold War (1947–1991)
  • US (capitalism/democracy) vs. USSR (communism/authoritarianism); no direct combat
  • Alliances: NATO (West) vs. Warsaw Pact (East)
  • Nuclear arms race: MAD doctrine (Mutually Assured Destruction)
  • Proxy wars: Korea (1950–53), Vietnam (1955–75), Angola, Afghanistan (1979–89)
  • Non-Aligned Movement: India (Nehru), Indonesia (Sukarno), Egypt (Nasser) refused to pick sides; Bandung Conference (1955)
  • End: Soviet economic stagnation + Afghanistan failure + Gorbachev reforms (glasnost = openness, perestroika = restructuring) → Berlin Wall falls (1989) → USSR dissolves (1991)

  • Israel founded (1948): ongoing Arab-Israeli conflict; Palestinian displacement
  • Chinese Revolution (1949): Mao + CCP → Great Leap Forward (famine, ~30M dead) → Cultural Revolution → Deng Xiaoping post-1978 market reforms

Unit 9: Globalization

c. 1900–present
Economic Globalization
  • Free-market reforms: Reagan (USA), Thatcher (UK), Deng Xiaoping (China) → deregulation, privatization
  • WTO (World Trade Organization) — governs global trade rules, reduce tariffs
  • NAFTA (1994) — free trade: USA, Canada, Mexico (now USMCA)
  • EU (European Union) — economic/political union; Euro currency; free movement
  • ASEAN — SE Asian economic cooperation
  • MNCs (multinational corporations) — shift manufacturing to Global South for cheap labor
  • Shipping containers (1950s) — standardized, dramatically cut shipping costs → global trade boom
Technology & Communication
  • Internet: connected world; instant information; e-commerce
  • Air travel: mass tourism; spread of diseases faster
  • Green Revolution (1940s–70s): high-yield crop varieties, fertilizers, irrigation → fed growing world population; Norman Borlaug
  • Medical advances: vaccines, antibiotics → ↑ life expectancy; global health organizations (WHO)
Disease in a Globalized World
  • HIV/AIDS pandemic: identified 1980s; global spread; Sub-Saharan Africa hardest hit
  • Ebola outbreaks: W. Africa; spread via global travel
  • COVID-19 (2019–present): rapid global spread via air travel; WHO declared pandemic
Environment & Climate
  • Deforestation: Amazon, SE Asia; driven by agriculture and logging
  • Desertification: Sahel region; overgrazing + climate change
  • Greenhouse gases: CO2 from industrialization → global warming
  • Kyoto Protocol (1997): nations commit to reduce emissions; US didn't ratify
  • Paris Agreement (2015): 195 nations; limit warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels
Resistance to Globalization
  • Anti-globalization protests: WTO Seattle (1999); argued MNCs exploit developing nations
  • Religious fundamentalism: reaction against Western secular culture; Islamic fundamentalism, Christian nationalism
  • Ethnic nationalism: preservation of local identity vs. homogenizing global culture
  • Local culture vs. Americanization: McDonaldization, Hollywood dominance resisted

Exam Strategy: DBQ · LEQ · SAQ · MC

Test Tips
DBQ (Document-Based Question) — 25%
  • 7 docs, 60 min (15 min reading + 45 min writing)
  • Thesis (1pt): must make a historically defensible claim beyond restating prompt; at least 2 sentences; appears in intro OR conclusion
  • Contextualization (1pt): describe broader historical context BEFORE or around the time of the prompt; must be a whole paragraph, not just a sentence
  • Evidence from docs: use 3 docs = 1pt; use 6+ docs = 2pts
  • Sourcing / HAPP (1pt): analyze Historical situation, Audience, Purpose, or Point of view for 3+ docs; explain HOW it affects content/reliability
  • Outside evidence (1pt): bring in specific fact NOT in the docs
  • Complexity (1pt): compare across time, geography, or scale; explain both cause AND effect; discuss both similarity AND difference
✓ During 15-min read: group docs by theme, draft thesis, note HAPP for each doc, plan your outside evidence before writing.
LEQ (Long Essay Question) — 15%
  • Choose 1 of 3 prompts, 40 min
  • Thesis (1pt): defensible claim with line of reasoning
  • Context (1pt): broad context before/around prompt
  • Evidence: 1 specific example = 1pt; 2+ explained examples = 2pts
  • Analysis (1pt): use historical reasoning (continuity/change over time, comparison, or causation)
  • Complexity (1pt): corroborate, qualify, or modify your argument
SAQ (Short Answer Question) — 20%
  • 3 questions, 40 min (Q1–2 have sources; Q3 or Q4 — choose 1, no source)
  • ACE method: Answer (the question directly), Cite evidence (specific name/date/event), Explain (the significance/connection)
  • Be SPECIFIC — name dates, people, places, and events. Vague = no points.
  • No thesis or intro needed; just answer A, B, C parts directly
Multiple Choice — 40%
  • 55 questions, 55 min (stimulus-based: map, text, image, graph)
  • Eliminate 2 obviously wrong → pick best remaining
  • Watch time-period boundaries — read question for "before 1750" vs. "after 1900"
  • Stimulus often shows a primary source; ask yourself: who wrote this, when, why?
Universal Thesis Formula
"[Although/While/Despite + counterargument], [your claim] because [reason 1], [reason 2], and [reason 3]."
Example: "Although the Mongols were destructive conquerors, they ultimately facilitated cross-cultural exchange by maintaining trade routes, diffusing technology, and promoting religious tolerance."
Historical Reasoning Skills
Causation — explain WHY something happened; causes + effects
Continuity & Change (CCOT) — what stayed the same AND what changed over time
Comparison — similarities AND differences across regions/time periods
Contextualization — connect to broader history BEFORE the event/period
Corroboration — connect multiple sources to strengthen an argument
Sourcing (HAPP) — Historical situation, Audience, Purpose, Point of view; HOW affects content
⚠ #1 mistake: VAGUE answers. Always name SPECIFIC people, dates, places, and events — not "some countries" or "many people."
